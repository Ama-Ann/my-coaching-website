The Compliment That Told Me I Wasn't Deep Enough Yet

Originally posted on LinkedIn · translated from Vietnamese

In 2019, more than ten years after the Managerial Skills training at Unilever from my last post, I stood in front of a classroom as a facilitator for a 3-day Leadership Skills course. The program devoted the entire final half-day to coaching, and the centerpiece was a live coaching demo — I played the coach, my co-facilitator colleague played the coachee, both of us trainers and coaches with real passion for coaching.

The thirty-minute demo, using the GROW model, flowed smoothly and naturally, without a single stumble. The participants watched, engaged, from start to finish. When it ended, the feedback they gave us was a compliment — but not the one I was expecting: “You two did it so well and so smoothly, it looked scripted! We're not sure we could ever do that.” The truth is, we weren't performing at all.

After years of practicing coaching, the GROW model, active listening, effective feedback — the basic techniques taught in any management course — felt like enough to me. A few times I sensed my coaching sessions weren't quite deep enough, and wondered if there was a more powerful tool than the GROW model, but I always let work sweep the thought away. That compliment didn't make me proud — it only made clearer something I had known for a long time but hadn't admitted: I was limited by the very thing I was most familiar with, just like in my own professional work.

That was the moment I realized I couldn't just keep circling coaching as a management tool. I needed to understand the real power of professional coaching, and what path would take me there. And that's when the real search began.

Have you ever been complimented on the exact thing you already knew you were lacking?

The Seed That Was Planted in a Managerial Skills Class

Originally posted on LinkedIn

Late 2009, a few months after the shock of a new hire quitting just one week into the job — the story from my last post — I attended a Managerial Skills class organized by Unilever Vietnam for mid-level managers. The trainer that day talked about one of the common reasons employees quit: their direct manager. Not necessarily because of bad treatment, but because the manager wasn't truly helping them grow. I sat there and suddenly understood something I had never thought about in quite this way.

I probably wasn't a bad boss, but I wasn't good enough. I hadn't been coaching my people, even though at the time, I genuinely believed I was supporting them as much as I could. That was the first time I heard the concept of coaching in a real work setting, not from a book or a theory, but like a missing piece sliding into exactly the gap I had.

I didn't know back then how far that moment would take me. Years later, when I decided to leave my role as a finance leader to become a professional coach, it turned out the first seed had been planted in a mid-level management class, at an FMCG company, on an afternoon in 2009. Has your first seed been planted yet?

Managing vs. Coaching: The Lesson From a Resignation

Originally posted on LinkedIn

2009. I hired a new team member — she had performed exceptionally well throughout the interview process. The role was anything but easy: treasury management for a global FMCG company with billion-dollar revenues, where even a small mistake could quickly become a big problem.

In her first week, the challenges came fast and hard. She would come to me with questions. I gave her clear, specific, step-by-step guidance. She nodded, went back to her desk. Then another difficult situation arose. She came back. I answered again. At the start of the following week, she didn't show up — “I'm overwhelmed. I'm afraid of making a mistake.”

This time, I was the one left stunned. I had been there for her every single time she needed me — so where did it go wrong? It took me a long time to realize: that was exactly the problem. I had always given her the answers — but not once had I helped her find the answers herself. Every time I “helped”, I was unknowingly telling her: “You are not capable of figuring this out on your own.” After hearing that enough times — even without a single word being said — she believed it.

Managing solves problems for people. Coaching helps people solve their own problems. One well-timed question can do what ten answers never could. Which one are you doing more of with your team?

“Give a man a fish, you feed him for a day. Teach a man to fish, you feed him for a lifetime.”

The CFO Message: How I Was Coaching Without Knowing It

Originally posted on LinkedIn

Recently, I received a message from someone I worked with over 10 years ago. She had just been promoted to CFO.

She didn't message just to share the news. She messaged to say thank you. I sat with that for a moment — what did I actually do for her back then? No structured training. No formal mentoring plan. She wasn't even on my team. Just listening. Asking questions. Trusting her enough to let her lead, even when I wasn't sure how things would turn out.

Looking back at the people who have crossed my path across many companies and industries — what I did most was not teach them. It was helping them find their own answers.

Back then, I had no intention of coaching her. But looking back now — with everything I have since learned, practiced, and trained in coaching — I realize that is exactly what I was doing, entirely by instinct. And that's why I'm here, wishing I had done more of it, much sooner.

Have you ever looked back and realized you were coaching someone — so naturally?
